1

The moment that changed everything

When I was a software developer, I used to sit in SAFe PI Planning sessions completely in awe. A Solutions Architect would stand up, deliver the Architecture Vision, and map out how an entire system would be built — before a single line of code was written. I remember thinking: how does someone see all of this so clearly, so early?

The way they talked about data flowing between systems reminded me of tracing data through a well-written function — but at ten times the scale. That moment sparked a real hunger in me. Not just to write great code inside one layer, but to understand how all the layers connect, and how better decisions at the architecture level make every developer's life easier downstream.

I wanted what I now call vertical breadth — an informed opinion across infrastructure, data, security, APIs, and business logic, all at once. This article is my honest account of what it actually takes to get there.

A word on imposter syndrome

If you're a senior developer reading this, you probably already have more of what it takes than you realise. The gap between senior developer and Solutions Architect is not a cliff — it's a gradual widening of perspective. This is a map, not a gate.

Next: what the role actually is — because it's widely misunderstood even inside tech teams.

2

What a Solutions Architect actually is

Not a senior developer who stopped coding. Not a PowerPoint architect who draws boxes and delegates everything. A Solutions Architect holds the technical thread from business problem all the way to running software — making sure every decision stays anchored to real needs.

Visionary
Translates ambiguous business problems into concrete technical solutions. Sees the end state before the team sees the first sprint.
Trade-off analyst
Weighs performance, cost, team capability, and long-term maintainability. Never picks a favourite technology — picks what fits best.
Communicator
Makes complex architecture legible to both engineers and business stakeholders. The same diagram must work for both audiences.
NFR guardian
Keeps non-functional requirements — performance, security, observability, cost — alive throughout the entire system lifecycle.
The T-shape principle

The best architects are T-shaped — deeply expert in at least one domain, broadly knowledgeable across all others. You don't need expertise in everything. You need enough in every area to ask the right questions and spot a bad decision early.

Now that we know the destination, let's map the realistic journey — in phases, not giant leaps.

3

Four phases of the journey

This transition unfolds gradually. Each phase has its own focus — and its own temptation to skip ahead before you're ready.

1–3y
Phase 1 — Build solid foundations
Years 1–3 · Software developer
Master one language deeply. Understand OS fundamentals, networking, and databases. Write clean, testable code. Contribute seriously to code reviews. The depth you build here is what gives your future architecture opinions credibility.
3–5y
Phase 2 — Expand across the stack
Years 3–5 · Senior developer / tech lead
Learn system design patterns. Own a feature end-to-end. Write your first ADR. Run a PoC. Study cloud platforms. Most importantly: practise explaining technical decisions to non-technical people — this skill accelerates everything else.
5–7y
Phase 3 — Think in systems
Years 5–7 · Principal / lead engineer
Design HLD and LLD architectures. Define NFRs. Drive technology strategy rather than just implement it. This is the phase where most people plateau — keep pushing through it.
7y+
Phase 4 — Architect
7+ years · Solutions Architect
Own the end-to-end architecture vision. Lead planning sessions. Mentor developers deliberately. Balance technical excellence with business value, budget, and timeline — all at once.
On timelines

These are indicative, not prescriptive. I've seen engineers make this shift in 5 years through deliberate practice. The calendar is irrelevant — intentional growth at every stage is what matters.

Phases tell you when. The next section tells you what to actually build.

Primary technical skills

These are the domains where an architect needs real competence — not expert-level mastery in every area, but enough depth to design with them, evaluate them, and recognise when something is wrong.

4.1 — OS & networking basics

You don't need to configure every router — but you must reason about why a system is slow, why a connection drops, and what happens when a certificate expires at 3am. These fundamentals are the invisible scaffolding behind every architecture decision.

DevSecOps means weaving development speed, operational reliability, and security into one continuous workflow. A system that ships fast but breaks often, or works well but is insecure, is a failure of architecture — not just operations.

4.4 — System design

System design is the architect's primary language. It has two layers — and keeping them separate is itself a skill.

High-Level Design (HLD)
The functional view — how components interact: microservices, APIs, databases, queues, caches. Non-technical stakeholders must be able to follow it. If they can't, it's not done.
Must-have
Low-Level Design (LLD)
The engineering view — class diagrams, sequence diagrams, schemas, API contracts. This is what the dev team implements from. Ambiguity here becomes bugs later.
Must-have

Technical skills sorted. Now the big section — thinking holistically about system quality before a single line of code is written.

5

Architecture Dossier — define this before you build anything

Think of the Architecture Dossier as the single source of truth for your system. It's the collection of quality targets, standards, and decisions that the whole team aligns to before building starts. Without it, every developer makes their own assumptions — and those assumptions quietly accumulate into technical debt, security gaps, and production surprises you can't explain.

The Dossier has four areas. Every architect must own all four.

5.1 — Quality targets (NFRs)

Developers think about what a system does. Architects think about how well it does it — and what happens when it doesn't. These quality properties shape every technology choice and every design decision that follows. Define them upfront, not after the first production incident.

Performance & latency
P99 response times, throughput targets, caching layers, CDN positioning. Define these before you pick a tech stack, not after you've already built it.
Scalability
Horizontal vs vertical scaling, stateless service design, auto-scaling policies, database sharding strategy for when traffic grows.
Reliability & availability
SLAs & SLOs, redundancy design, multi-region failover, and chaos engineering practices to test resilience before customers do.
Security
AuthN/AuthZ, encryption at rest and in transit, OWASP Top 10 mitigations, and zero-trust principles for access control.
Maintainability
Code quality gates, dependency management, backward-compatible API versioning, and semantic versioning standards that keep upgrades predictable.
Cost efficiency
Cloud right-sizing, reserved vs spot instances, resource tagging strategy, cost alerting thresholds, and FinOps practices to keep the bill from shocking you.
Deployability
Blue-green, canary, or rolling deploys. Feature flags. Rollback triggers. Smoke test criteria decided before the first release, not during it.
5.2 — Testing strategy

Architects define the testing pyramid for the whole system. Getting this wrong is expensive — too many E2E tests and your pipeline becomes slow and brittle; too few integration tests and production surprises you constantly.

Unit tests
Fast, isolated, developer-owned. Cover every branch. Your cheapest safety net — invest heavily here first.
Foundation
Integration tests
Verify components work together correctly. Test contracts between them, not internal implementations.
Foundation
End-to-end tests
Simulate complete user journeys. Keep the suite small — focused on the most critical business flows only.
Strategic
Contract tests
Validate API contracts between services (e.g. Pact). Essential in microservices to catch breaking changes before they reach production.
Advanced
5.3 — Observability

Once your code is live in production, how do you know it's healthy? Observability is the practice of making your system's internal state visible from the outside — so that when something goes wrong (and it will), you can debug it fast instead of guessing in the dark.

Structured logging
Every service logs in a consistent, machine-readable format (JSON). Logs tell you what happened. Ship them to a centralised store like ELK Stack or Loki. Unstructured log strings are impossible to query at scale.
Distributed tracing
Traces tell you where a request went — across every service it touched. Tools like Jaeger or Zipkin, built on OpenTelemetry, let you follow one request through 10 microservices in a single view.
Metrics & dashboards
Metrics tell you how much — request rate, error rate, latency. Prometheus + Grafana is the most common open-source pairing. Build your dashboards before go-live, not after the first incident.
Alerting strategy
Alert on symptoms, not causes. Alert when an SLO is at risk — not when CPU hits 70%. Every alert must have a clear owner, a runbook link, and a defined response time.
The three pillars of observability

Logs tell you what happened. Traces tell you where the request went. Metrics tell you how the system is behaving over time. Design for all three before you deploy — not after you're debugging a live incident with no visibility.

5.4 — Documentation

An architect who doesn't document is creating institutional debt. Every significant decision should leave a written record that future engineers can read without needing to ask anyone.

Architecture Decision Records (ADRs)

The most important document an architect can write. Captures not just what you decided but why — including the options you considered and rejected. Future engineers will inherit your choices; the ADR is how they understand your reasoning instead of cursing your decisions.

Example ADR — Choosing a message broker for the event pipeline
Title
ADR-004: Use Apache Kafka for the telemetry event pipeline
Status
Accepted
Context
We need to process 50k+ events/second from distributed sensors with replay capability and consumer group isolation.
Decision
Use Apache Kafka — supports high throughput, log compaction, and consumer groups natively.
Alternatives
RabbitMQ — rejected (no log replay) · AWS SQS — rejected (limited throughput) · Redis Streams — rejected (insufficient maturity at this scale)
Consequences
Higher operational overhead. Team needs Kafka training. Long-term benefit in replay and audit capability.

Other documents every system needs

Deployment strategy doc
Defines the release approach — blue-green, canary, rolling. Includes rollback triggers, smoke test criteria, and who signs off on go-live. Write this before the first release.
SOP (Standard Operating Procedure)
Step-by-step runbooks for deployments, incident response, certificate rotation. Written clearly enough for anyone to follow at 3am under pressure.
User guide for business
Non-technical documentation in plain language explaining how the system supports business workflows. Stakeholders who understand the system trust you more and ask better questions.
Threat model
Identifies attack surfaces and mitigations. Even a lightweight STRIDE analysis at architecture phase adds enormous security value at minimal cost.
The Dossier is a living document

It's not written once and filed away. It evolves as the system evolves. Every ADR, every deployment decision, every observability threshold you define is a record of how you think. The team reads it when you're not in the room — make sure it speaks clearly on your behalf.

Dossier covered. Now the most important section — the on-the-job skills no course, cert, or side project can teach you.

6

On-the-job reality — what only the role teaches

Some skills you can study and simulate. Others only crystallise when the business is looking at you for answers, the budget is real, and five teams are waiting on your decision. No tutorial replicates that.

The Solutions Architect role asks you to wear several hats across a project's lifecycle — not all at once, but fluidly. You don't own every role, but you provide the technical perspective across all of them.

The flow: from business problem to delivered software

1
Functional Architect hat
Understand the business first — before touching technology
Resist the urge to jump to a solution. Sit with the business, listen carefully, and build a Functional Diagram — a technology-agnostic picture of the business flow, the actors, the data, and the outcomes. Until the business can look at this and say "yes, that's our world," you haven't earned the right to start designing a system.
Business workshopsFunctional diagramsActor mappingDomain modelling
2
Solutions Architect hat
Translate functional requirements into systems and technology
Only after the functional picture is clear do you put on the technical lens. You produce a High-Level Architecture — the services, integrations, data stores, and APIs that will deliver the outcome. This HLD becomes the north star for every technical decision that follows.
HLD diagramTechnology selectionNFR definitionPoC recommendations
3
Estimator hat
Break the work into bricks — estimate by deliverable, not by project
Estimating the whole project as one number is a trap. Decompose the architecture into deliverables — each one an MVP-sized brick of working value. Estimate each independently: scope, effort, dependencies, risk. This keeps delivery honest and surfaces trade-offs before they become crises.
MVP decompositionWork breakdownEffort estimationRisk sizing
4
Budget advisor hat
Give the business a budget — broken down by deliverable
Each deliverable gets a budget line. This is architecture work, not finance work. Architects who understand the cost of their choices — cloud infra, licensing, team effort — earn a level of leadership trust that purely technical architects never achieve.
Cloud cost modellingLicence & tooling costsTrade-off advisory
5
Product Manager hat
Fit deliverables into a roadmap — give the business a "when"
Sequence your MVPs into a roadmap the business can commit to: which bricks land in which quarter, what dependencies must be resolved first, what the critical path is. The business now has a real answer to "when are we getting this?" — anchored in technical reality, not wishful thinking.
Roadmap sequencingCritical pathMilestone definition
6
Orchestrator hat
Break the roadmap into features and stories — hand it to the team
Each roadmap item gets broken into features and user stories the dev team can execute. Your LLD, API contracts, and architecture diagrams become the team's working specifications. The quality of this handoff determines how faithfully the system gets built.
Epic decompositionStory mappingArchitecture handoff
7
Governance hat
Track, review, and keep the thread intact
You're not the Scrum Master or the RTE — but you review completed deliverables against the original architecture, flag drift before it becomes debt, and provide the technical narrative in milestone reviews.
Architecture reviewDrift detectionRisk escalation
The most important distinction

You wear many hats — but you don't own all the roles. The PM, Scrum Master, and RTE each own their domain. Your job is to provide the technical opinion across all of them. You are the connective tissue between the business problem and delivered software.

7

Secondary skills — the career multipliers

These rarely appear in job descriptions. But they separate architects who are technically correct from those who actually drive change.

Presentation & storytelling
Your architecture is only as good as your ability to make others believe in it. Every explanation needs a "so what" — why this decision matters to the business, not just the system.
Critical
Structured curiosity
A disciplined habit of going deep on one new topic each month and documenting the takeaways. Blogs, PoCs, and talks are the visible output of this curiosity.
Critical
Stakeholder management
Aligning developers, PMs, business owners, and security teams — all with different priorities. Learn to negotiate constraints rather than just design around them.
Important
Technical writing
Clear documentation outlasts any verbal explanation. Master the ADR format, RFC structure, and the art of writing a runbook someone can follow at 3am under stress.
Important
Mentoring
The architect who lifts junior developers up multiplies their own impact exponentially. The best technical leaders are also the best teachers in the room.
Bonus
Domain knowledge
Understanding the business domain you build for lets you challenge requirements — not just implement them. Business fluency is a genuine competitive advantage.
Bonus
8

Certifications — good to have, not enough alone

Certifications don't make you an architect — but they signal commitment, validate your cloud fundamentals, and force you to fill knowledge gaps that project work alone might never expose.

Associate · AWS
AWS Solutions Architect Associate
Your entry point into cloud architecture thinking. Covers compute, storage, networking, IAM, and core services. Start here — it restructures how you think about infrastructure design.
Professional · AWS
AWS Solutions Architect Professional
Advanced multi-account architecture, complex migrations, and enterprise integration patterns. This is the cert that earns credibility in architecture conversations.
Associate · HashiCorp
Terraform Associate
Validates IaC fundamentals — provisioning, state, modules, workspaces. Infrastructure as code is no longer optional for architects who want to stay relevant.
The cert trap

A certification without hands-on experience is a badge on a wall. Earn the cert and immediately apply it — provision real infrastructure with Terraform, architect a project in AWS, and document your decisions publicly. The cert opens the door; your portfolio gets you the role.

9

Building your personal brand in public

This is the highest-ROI career investment on this list — and the one most engineers skip because it feels uncomfortable or self-promotional. It's neither. When you document your learning consistently and publicly, something shifts: recruiters and companies begin coming to you. You stop applying for roles — opportunities find you.

Write articles on Medium
Publish every project, learning, and architectural decision. Even short "what I learned" posts compound over time into public proof of expertise no CV can replicate.
Credibility + discoverability
Record videos on YouTube
Explain your projects — how the system works, how the code is structured, why you made the decisions you made. Authenticity and clarity matter more than production quality.
Reach + trust
Live coding & building on Instagram, YouTube
Share the messy parts — debugging, searching, figuring things out in real time. The gap between polished tutorials and reality is where people are most stuck, and where you can help most.
Community + authenticity
Document every repo on GitHub
Every project needs a comprehensive README, architecture diagram, setup guide, and decision log. A well-documented repo is a silent portfolio piece working for you around the clock.
Portfolio + hiring signal
The compounding effect

Documenting your work doesn't only build your brand — it sharpens your thinking. Writing and teaching forces deeper understanding than doing alone. You'll also develop skills you didn't plan on: video editing, article writing, communication, and teaching. You give back to the community — and the community gives back to your career in ways you can't predict.

10

Mindset shift + self-assessment checklist

Acquiring new technical knowledge is the easy part. The hard part is unlearning the expert's instinct — the tendency to favour the tools you know over the tools that fit. This shift takes deliberate, sustained effort.

Developer mindset
"How do I implement this?"
"What is the most elegant solution?"
"Which tool is technically superior here?"
"Let me pick the best technology for this module."
Architect mindset
"Should we build this, buy it, or use an existing service?"
"What happens when this fails at 3am under peak load?"
"What does this decision cost us in three years?"
"What can our team actually operate well?"
The most common trap

Over-engineering. The best architecture is the simplest one that reliably meets the NFRs. Complexity is a liability, not a demonstration of expertise. When in doubt, choose boring, proven technology your team already understands. Novelty is not a feature.

"An architect's greatest tool is not a diagram. It is a well-formed question asked at exactly the right moment."

Are you ready? An honest checklist

Not a gate — a diagnostic. Use it to find where to invest next.

I can sketch a complete HLD from a 30-minute requirements conversation on a system I've never worked on before
I've written at least one ADR that was accepted and implemented by a real team
I've presented a technical architecture to a non-technical executive and answered their follow-up questions without retreating to jargon
I've run a PoC, documented the results, and recommended against my original assumption based on evidence
I can define the complete NFR profile of a system I own — SLAs, security posture, deployment strategy, cost model
I've set up observability for a production system — structured logging, distributed tracing, metrics dashboards, and alerting
I've built or contributed to a CI/CD pipeline with containerisation and IaC from the ground up
I've produced a Functional Diagram with business stakeholders before drawing any system architecture
I've estimated a project by deliverable — with scope, effort, and budget — and stood behind those estimates in a business review
I've documented a project publicly (repo, article, or video) and know that someone else learned from it
I've disagreed with a technology or design decision in writing, with evidence and alternatives, and defended it
I understand the business domain I build for well enough to challenge a requirement — not just implement it

The journey from developer to architect is not a ladder with a top rung. It's a widening lens. The code never stops mattering — but the picture you hold it up to gets larger, more complex, and more consequential every year. The more generously you share what you see through that lens, the further your career and impact will go.

"I didn't want just horizontal depth of knowledge. I wanted vertical overview — across infrastructure, data, application, and business layers — all at once. That hunger is still what drives me."
